Coilover Spring Rates: OEM vs Aftermarket Track Geometry

PRP Engineering Team

Updated: June 18, 2026

8 min read

Upgrading a vehicle's suspension from OEM MacPherson struts to a fully adjustable true-coilover system is not just about lowering the ride height—it is about fundamentally altering the chassis' Roll Center and roll-moment geometry to maximize tire contact patches under lateral load.

In this engineering breakdown, we explore why linear spring rates in premium aftermarket kits outperform OEM progressive springs during track conditions.

1. Progressive vs. Linear Spring Rates

OEM manufacturers are obsessed with NVH (Noise, Vibration, and Harshness). To keep the daily commute comfortable, they use progressive springs. These springs are soft at the top of their travel to absorb bumps, but get stiffer as they compress.

The problem? Unpredictability. Mid-corner, if you hit a dip, the spring rate rapidly changes, unsettling the chassis and breaking traction.

Premium high-ticket coilovers (like BC Racing or KW Suspensions) use linear springs. If a spring is rated at 8kg/mm, it takes exactly 8kg to compress it one millimeter, and exactly 16kg to compress it two millimeters. This mathematical predictability allows drivers to tune their dampening to perfection.

2. Independent Ride Height Adjustment

Cheap "lowering springs" compress the shock absorber piston simply by being shorter, reducing your suspension travel and leading to "bottoming out."

True high-performance coilovers feature an adjustable lower mount. This allows you to thread the entire shock body down into the lower cup. You lower the car's center of gravity without losing a single millimeter of shock travel or altering the spring preload.
